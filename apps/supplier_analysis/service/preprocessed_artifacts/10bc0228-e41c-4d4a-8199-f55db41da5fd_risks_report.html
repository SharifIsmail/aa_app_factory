Shell Petroleum Development Company of Nigeria Ltd

Supplier Risk Assessment

Executive Summary

Shell Petroleum Development Company of Nigeria Limited (SPDC) operates in one of the most complex and high-risk environments in the world. Although Shell has a long-standing presence in Nigeria and a sophisticated governance framework globally, its subsidiary SPDC faces severe challenges related to labor conditions, safety, environmental damage, and corruption in the Niger Delta. Numerous documented violations and systemic issues require careful attention. SPDC is classified as a high-risk supplier in this assessment and should be subject to enhanced due diligence and continuous monitoring.

Supplier Information

  • Name: Shell Petroleum Development Company of Nigeria Limited (SPDC)
  • Ownership: Joint Venture operated by Shell plc (30%) with NNPC (55%), TotalEnergies (10%), Eni (5%)
  • Headquarters: Port Harcourt, Rivers State, Nigeria
  • Operations: Active in the Niger Delta, with 6,000+ km of pipelines, 87 flow stations, 8 gas plants
  • Business Profile: Upstream oil and gas exploration, extraction, processing, and export
  • Key Director: Osagie Okunbor (Managing Director & Country Chair)
  • Years Active: Since 1936 in Nigeria; over 85 years of operations

Key Risk Factors

Labor Rights (8/10)

Reports from labor groups and investigative journalists show SPDC relies heavily on low-paid contractors with minimal protections. Workers face dismissal threats for unionizing, with inadequate benefits and grievance mechanisms. Allegations indicate systemic labor abuses despite Shell’s global human rights policy.

Safety Violations (8/10)

The Niger Delta is a high-risk zone due to pipeline sabotage, oil theft, and community unrest. SPDC staff and contractors have died in blasts, and contract workers report poor training and safety gear. Kidnappings and threats are also persistent issues.

Environmental Issues (10/10)

SPDC is one of the largest contributors to oil pollution in Nigeria. Thousands of spills have devastated ecosystems and human health. Legal actions have resulted in billion-naira fines and international settlements. Critics accuse Shell of failing to clean up legacy damage promptly or transparently.

Financial & Corruption (9/10)

Shell’s operations in Nigeria have been connected to massive corruption allegations, most notably the OPL 245 scandal. Although acquitted in Italy, the reputational risk and regulatory scrutiny remain high. Nigeria's oil sector presents a systemic corruption risk that affects all major players.

Country & Industry Risk Overview

Country Risk – Nigeria (8/10)

Nigeria presents a highly complex risk environment, especially in extractive sectors:

  • Corruption: The petroleum sector has been the center of major corruption scandals. Regulatory opacity and political interference are prevalent.
  • Security: Armed militancy, oil theft, and sabotage of energy infrastructure are endemic, particularly in the Niger Delta.
  • Human Rights: Documented cases of repression, police violence, and weak judicial enforcement affect communities and worker rights.
  • Governance: Environmental remediation and social accountability mechanisms are under-resourced and inconsistently applied.

While Nigeria remains a critical energy hub, the reputational and legal risks are elevated, requiring suppliers to adopt strict controls and transparent engagement strategies.

Industry Risk – Oil & Gas (Upstream) (9/10)

The upstream petroleum industry, especially in Nigeria, faces intense scrutiny and material risks:

  • Environmental Risk: Oil spills, gas flaring, and habitat destruction are frequent; remediation efforts often lag behind impact.
  • Social Conflict: Operations in indigenous and marginalized communities fuel longstanding tensions, protests, and litigation.
  • Worker Safety: Operations involve hazardous materials, insecure areas, and risks from sabotage and unrest.
  • Global Pressure: ESG investors, courts, and NGOs increasingly target oil majors over their conduct in frontier markets.

Any engagement with upstream oil & gas suppliers in the Niger Delta should proceed with robust contractual safeguards, public accountability, and third-party verification.

Conclusion & Recommended Actions

Shell Petroleum Development Company of Nigeria Ltd (SPDC) is classified as a high-risk supplier across all major categories of the LkSG Risk Assessment framework. While it is a strategic and capable partner in the energy space, the depth of environmental, social, and governance risks calls for enhanced due diligence, strict contract clauses, and routine on-the-ground monitoring.

  • Mandate supplier-level social audits and environmental reporting with independent verification
  • Implement ongoing community engagement and impact tracking for operations in the Niger Delta
  • Review anti-corruption and compliance systems annually
  • Apply escalation pathways per LkSG rules if significant incidents occur or legal rulings are issued
  • Review the SPDC risk profile every 6–12 months
