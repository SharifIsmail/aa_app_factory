Country & Industry Risk Overview

Country Risk – Nigeria (6/10)

Nigeria presents a mixed risk profile for suppliers. It has a dynamic economy and a growing population, but it also faces challenges such as:

  • Corruption: Ranked low in global anti-corruption indices, with risks in public procurement and local governance.
  • Security: Regional security issues, especially in the North, and infrastructure disruptions can affect logistics and operations.
  • Regulatory Environment: Complex bureaucracy and inconsistent enforcement of laws can pose operational challenges.
  • Currency Volatility: The Naira has been unstable, with limited access to foreign exchange in some sectors.

While Lagos is one of the more stable business hubs, operating in Nigeria still requires careful due diligence and risk mitigation strategies.

Industry Risk – Interior Design & Construction (4/10)

The interior design and furnishing industry is generally low-risk in terms of environmental and labor exploitation concerns, but presents some moderate challenges:

  • Informality: Many players operate informally or with limited documentation and compliance infrastructure.
  • Safety Hazards: Risks exist from tools, scaffolding, and exposure to chemicals (e.g., paints, adhesives).
  • Labor Standards: Often project-based work with temporary staff, raising concerns about fair wages and benefits.
  • Sustainability: Environmental impact can arise from improper disposal of waste and use of non-eco-friendly materials.

For suppliers in this sector, it is crucial to assess their safety protocols, training practices, and environmental commitments—even if on a small scale.

Recommended Actions

  • Obtain and Verify Company Documentation: Request official documents such as CAC Certificate of Incorporation, form CO7/CAC directors' list, and any available financial statements or bank reference letters.
  • Conduct Financial Due Diligence: Ask for recent financial statements or references. Consider requiring a performance bond or bank guarantee for significant contracts.
  • Evaluate Operational Capacity: Seek information on their portfolio and client references. Visit a current project site or completed project to see their work quality and safety practices.
  • Require Compliance Commitments: Have the supplier sign a Supplier Code of Conduct covering labor standards, health & safety, environmental care, and anti-corruption.
  • Monitor Labor and Safety Practices: Conduct a site visit or audit early in the contract. Check working conditions and safety procedures.
  • Environmental Oversight: Ask how they plan to handle waste and whether any hazardous substances will be used. Require an environmental management plan proportional to the project.
  • Strengthen Anti-Corruption Measures: Communicate anti-bribery expectations clearly. Consider conducting a brief compliance training for their management.
  • Ongoing Monitoring: Treat the first contract as a probation period. Monitor performance closely and have regular check-ins.
  • Collaborate on Improvements: For long-term relationships, encourage them to pursue relevant certifications once the partnership is established.
  • Document and Cover Data Gaps: Acknowledge in internal risk files where information was lacking and keep records of steps taken to mitigate these gaps.
